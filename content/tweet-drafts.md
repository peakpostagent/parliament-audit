## 1. Explainer Series: "What is a recorded vote?"

**Hook:**
> Canada's Parliament holds hundreds of votes a year. Most pass without names attached — but some are "recorded divisions" where every MP's vote is logged.
>
> These are the votes that matter. Here's why. 🧵

**Thread body:**

> 1/ A recorded division happens when 5+ MPs request one, or on key bills at second and third reading.
>
> The Speaker calls names. Each MP stands to vote yea, nay, or pair. The full tally gets published on ourcommons.ca.

> 2/ This is the only time the public sees *who* voted for what — not just *what* passed.
>
> Party whips usually enforce a line. But "free votes" let MPs break ranks. Those are the ones worth watching.

> 3/ Yet most Canadians never hear about them. By the time a vote makes the news, it's framed by a press release.
>
> Parliament Audit publishes every recorded vote within hours, with a plain-language explainer. No spin.
>
> parliamentaudit.ca

## 2. Explainer Series: "How a bill becomes law"

**Hook:**
> Your local MP probably voted on a dozen bills last month. Do you know how many times?
>
> A Canadian bill faces 8+ recorded moments where MPs' names go on record. Here's the map. 🧵

**Thread body:**

> 1/ First reading: the bill is tabled. No vote.

> 2/ Second reading: MPs vote on the *principle* of the bill. Pass = it goes to committee. Fail = it dies.

> 3/ Committee: clause-by-clause. Amendments get recorded votes when contested.

> 4/ Report stage: MPs vote on each amendment the committee made. This is where late-stage changes happen.

> 5/ Third reading: final vote in the House. Pass = it goes to the Senate.

> 6/ Senate: repeat steps 2–5 in the other chamber.

> 7/ Royal Assent: the Governor General signs it into law.
>
> Every one of those recorded votes is searchable at parliamentaudit.ca. By MP. By party. By bill.

## 3. Explainer Series: "What 'party discipline' means"

**Hook:**
> When a Canadian MP votes against their own party, it's called "breaking the whip."
>
> In most parliaments, it happens <2% of the time. Here's why — and which votes tend to be free. 🧵

**Thread body:**

> 1/ Most votes are "whipped": the party leadership tells MPs how to vote. Break ranks and you can lose committee seats, cabinet roles, or nomination support.

> 2/ Free votes are the exception. Conscience issues (abortion, assisted dying), private members' bills, and some local-interest motions are often unwhipped.

> 3/ Between the extremes sits the "three-line whip" system used informally in Canada — ranging from "vote however" to "vote or resign."

> 4/ We track every MP's record so you can see when they voted with — and against — their caucus.
>
> Search any MP at parliamentaudit.ca/find-your-mp
