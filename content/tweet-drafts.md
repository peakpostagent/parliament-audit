# Parliament Audit — Tweet Drafts (Journalism Framework)

All drafts below are **for your review only** — nothing posts until you approve.

## Editorial Framework

Every tweet follows this structure:

1. **Hook** — what just happened (lead with the news)
2. **Fact** — who did what, the decision or vote result
3. **Why it matters** — impact on taxpayers, civil rights, or transparency
4. **Contrast** (optional) — past statements vs. current actions
5. **CTA** — link to the full article on parliamentaudit.ca

## Voice Rules (non-negotiable)

- Non-partisan. Report the record. Never editorialize.
- Factual. Every claim sourced or sourceable from Hansard, LEGISinfo, or official records.
- No loaded language ("slams", "crushes", "betrays", "shocking"). Use neutral verbs.
- Lead with what happened, not what you think about it.
- Numbers over narrative. Data over drama.
- Always link to parliamentaudit.ca for the full story.
- No partisan hashtags. #cdnpoli is acceptable. Nothing that endorses or attacks a party or leader.
- Attribute actions to institutions and individuals by name, not vague references.

## Template 1: Breaking Vote

**Use when:** A recorded division happens in the House or Senate.

**Format:**
> RECORDED VOTE — [BILL #]: [one-line description of what it does]
>
> Result: [PASSED/FAILED] [yeas]–[nays]
>
> [One sentence on why this matters for Canadians.]
>
> Full breakdown by party and MP: parliamentaudit.ca/vote/[slug]

**Rules:**
- Only post after the article is published and fact-checked.
- Lead with the result, not the partisan angle.
- Include the vote tally. The numbers are the hook.

---

## Template 2: Bill Explainer

**Use when:** Legislation needs a plain-language breakdown.

**Format:**
> [BILL #] would [plain-language description of what the bill does].
>
> [One sentence: who introduced it, current status.]
>
> Here's what it means for [affected group — taxpayers / privacy / healthcare / etc.]:
>
> parliamentaudit.ca/[slug]

**Rules:**
- Describe what the bill does in concrete terms, not abstractions.
- Name the sponsor and the stage (second reading, committee, etc.).
- Avoid legal jargon. If a 17-year-old can't parse it, rewrite it.

---

## Template 3: Accountability Check

**Use when:** An MP's or party's past public statements contradict their current actions or votes.

**Format:**
> [DATE/CONTEXT]: [MP or Party] said "[direct quote or paraphrase with source]."
>
> [DATE]: [MP or Party] voted [yea/nay] on [BILL #], which does [the opposite / something inconsistent].
>
> The record: parliamentaudit.ca/[slug]

**Rules:**
- Both the statement and the vote must be verifiable from public sources (Hansard, press conferences, official party platforms).
- Never speculate on motives. Present the two facts side by side and let readers draw conclusions.
- Rotate parties. Do not run consecutive accountability checks on the same party.

---

## Template 4: Budget / Spending

**Use when:** Government spending decisions, budget votes, or fiscal policy stories.

**Format:**
> [$ amount] — that's how much [the government just allocated / cut from / committed to] [program or area].
>
> [One sentence: what this means in practical terms — jobs, services, timelines.]
>
> Where the money goes: parliamentaudit.ca/[slug]

**Rules:**
- Always use specific dollar figures. Round to the nearest billion or million as appropriate.
- Provide context: per-capita, year-over-year change, or comparison to related spending.
- Avoid adjectives like "massive" or "tiny." Let the number speak.

---

## Template 5: Weekly Recap

**Use when:** End-of-week summary (post Saturdays or Sundays).

**Format — sitting week:**
> This week in Parliament:
>
> - [N] recorded votes in the House
> - [Key bill] advanced to [stage]
> - [Notable event: floor crossing, committee finding, etc.]
>
> The full week, vote by vote: parliamentaudit.ca/archive

**Format — recess:**
> Parliament is in recess. No recorded votes this week.
>
> Next sitting: [date]. On the order paper: [key bills expected].
>
> Catch up on what you missed: parliamentaudit.ca/archive

**Rules:**
- Lead with the most consequential item, not the most numerous.
- Keep to 3 bullet points maximum. Link out for depth.

---

## Template 6: Data Drop

**Use when:** Interesting statistics or patterns emerge from parliamentary records.

**Format:**
> [Stat or data point presented as a single striking sentence.]
>
> [One sentence of context: time period, comparison, or trend.]
>
> We pulled this from [source]. Full data: parliamentaudit.ca/[slug]

**Rules:**
- The stat must be independently verifiable.
- Provide the time range and sample size so readers can assess significance.
- Do not cherry-pick. If the trend reversed recently, note that.
