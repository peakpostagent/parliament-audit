# Day 3 (Wed) — The Privacy Commissioner is missing from Bill C-22

**Slug:** `bill-c-22-privacy-commissioner-no-oversight-role`
**Category:** `Legislation`
**Angle:** the structural / institutional piece. The Office of the
Privacy Commissioner is the federal body that exists specifically to
audit how government and providers handle Canadians' personal data.
The bill grants new powers and creates new retention mandates — and
contains no statutory role for the OPC. That absence is itself
a story.

## Headline

> Bill C-22 Creates New Surveillance Powers. The Privacy Commissioner Has No Role in Overseeing Any of Them.

## Subheadline

> Every recent Canadian lawful-access proposal — from Bill C-30 in 2012 to Bill C-2 last year — included some statutory role for the Office of the Privacy Commissioner. Bill C-22 does not. The OPC cannot audit how the retained metadata is stored, cannot review the secret capability orders, and has no investigation power over complaints arising from the new regime. Here is what changed.

## Smart Brevity

- **bigThing**: The Office of the Privacy Commissioner has no statutory oversight role under Bill C-22 — no audit power, no review of secret orders, no complaint jurisdiction over the new surveillance regime.
- **whyItMatters**: The OPC is the federal body specifically designed to audit privacy-affecting government and private-sector practices. Its absence from a privacy-affecting bill is a deliberate design choice, not an oversight.
- **goDeeper**:
  - Bill C-30 (2012, withdrawn): contained OPC reporting requirements.
  - Bill C-2 (2025, abandoned): contained OPC reporting requirements.
  - Bill C-22 (2026): contains no OPC oversight role.
  - Michael Geist: the absence "suggests that privacy is at best a secondary consideration" in the bill's design.
  - Department of Justice Charter statement on C-22: silent on metadata retention.
- **yesBut**: The bill does require ministerial orders under Part 2 (SAAIA) to receive Intelligence Commissioner approval. That is a different review body, with a different scope.
- **bottomLine**: The Intelligence Commissioner reviews ministerial decisions for reasonableness; the Privacy Commissioner audits privacy practices in detail. They are not interchangeable. The bill substitutes one for the other.

## Sections (outline)

1. **What the OPC does** — brief: the Privacy Commissioner is an Officer of Parliament who audits federal privacy practices, investigates complaints, reports to Parliament, and has been the primary federal privacy watchdog since 1983.

2. **What other lawful-access bills included** — Bill C-30 (the 2012 Toews bill) and Bill C-2 (2025) both included OPC reporting requirements. Quote the relevant clauses for comparison.

3. **What C-22 contains** — read the bill text. There is no provision granting the OPC statutory authority over: metadata retention practices, secret capability orders, the SAAIA regime as a whole. The OPC retains its general PIPEDA jurisdiction over private-sector providers' handling of metadata, but no specific role over the C-22 regime.

4. **What the Intelligence Commissioner does and doesn't do** — the bill points to the Intelligence Commissioner as the review body for ministerial orders. But: the IC reviews specific ministerial decisions for reasonableness on a case-by-case basis; the IC does not audit retention practices, investigate complaints, or report publicly to Parliament. The roles are not interchangeable.

5. **What experts have said** — Geist's "primary consideration" framing. The Charter statement gap. The Onion Mail / privacy advocacy framing of "second-generation surveillance" precisely because the oversight architecture is weaker than predecessors.

6. **What an OPC role could look like** — describe what an amendment would add. The OPC could be given audit power over retention practices, an obligation to be notified of secret orders (in confidence), and the ability to report aggregate statistics to Parliament. None of this is hypothetical — it's all standard for OPC oversight of other federal regimes.

7. **The political math** — for the OPC role to be added, a Liberal MP on the SECU committee would have to propose or accept the amendment. Six Liberals sit on SECU. Three opposition members. The committee chair is by convention from the governing party.
