# Day 2 (Tue) — A year of metadata: what it actually reveals

**Slug:** `bill-c-22-what-one-year-of-metadata-reveals`
**Category:** `Legislation`
**Angle:** vivid concrete piece that turns "metadata retention" from an
abstract phrase into something a layperson actually feels. The bill's
explicit exclusion of "content of communications" is technically true
and rhetorically misleading — metadata alone reveals enormous detail.

## Headline

> Bill C-22 Doesn't Read Your Texts. It Doesn't Have To.

## Subheadline

> The bill explicitly excludes the content of communications from mandatory retention. What it would require — who you talked to, when, where you were, what device you used — is the data that intelligence professionals call "the more important half" of surveillance. Here is what a year of that data reveals about an ordinary Canadian.

## Smart Brevity

- **bigThing**: Bill C-22 mandates the retention of metadata, not message content. But metadata is what intelligence services call "the more important half" of surveillance — it reveals associations, movements, beliefs, and patterns of life.
- **whyItMatters**: The bill's defenders point to the content exclusion as a privacy safeguard. The intelligence community has been on the record for over a decade saying the opposite: that metadata is more useful than content for understanding a person.
- **goDeeper**:
  - Who you called and when (every call, every duration).
  - Every cell tower your phone connected to (location every 5–15 minutes, year-round).
  - Every device you used and the unique identifier of each one.
  - Every IP address you connected to (which website, which app, which service).
  - The pattern: by week 6 of analyzed metadata, a competent analyst can usually identify your job, your sleep schedule, your religion, who you live with, your sexual orientation, and your political associations.
- **yesBut**: The bill states law enforcement still needs a production order to access this data, with a "reasonable grounds to suspect" threshold. Content access still requires a full search warrant.
- **bottomLine**: The choice of words in the bill — "metadata, not content" — is technically accurate and substantively misleading. The retention IS the surveillance; the access threshold is a second question.

## Key takeaways

- Metadata = who, when, where, how. Not what was said.
- Stuart Baker (former NSA general counsel, 2014): "Metadata absolutely tells you everything about somebody's life. If you have enough metadata, you don't really need content."
- Michael Hayden (former director of NSA and CIA, 2014): "We kill people based on metadata."
- The retention applies to every Canadian — not just suspects.
- Even with high access thresholds, the existence of a year-long metadata trail changes what is knowable about every Canadian.

## Sections (outline)

1. **What the bill says metadata is** — quote the bill text: transmission data, date/time/duration/type, device identifiers, location information. Quote the bill's exclusion of content.

2. **What metadata actually reveals** — the Baker and Hayden quotes (both from 2014 in public, on-record settings). The Stanford "MetaPhone" study from 2014 that showed 5 days of phone metadata is enough to identify medical conditions, religious affiliation, sexual relationships, gun ownership.

3. **A worked example: a year in the life of an ordinary Canadian** — walk through what could be derived from 12 months of metadata for a person who: works in a city, has a chronic illness, attends religious services, is having an affair, is involved in a union, votes for a particular party. Not as accusation — as illustration. (Important framing note: present this as "metadata reveals these things about anyone, not as 'the government would use this to target you.'")

4. **What the intelligence community has said about metadata vs. content** — quotes from former NSA, CIA, GCHQ officials. The Snowden disclosures on what NSA could derive from "metadata only" databases.

5. **Why the content-exclusion language in C-22 may be doing rhetorical work** — Geist's observation that excluding content is a politically safer pitch than the underlying retention is in substance. The Charter statement is silent on metadata-specific harms.

6. **What this means for ordinary Canadians** — the practical change: today, your metadata is held by your provider for billing-cycle periods that vary. Tomorrow, under C-22, that data exists for at least a year on every Canadian, retrievable on a court order with a lower threshold than a warrant.
