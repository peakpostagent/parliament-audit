# Day 6 (Sat) — Documented Canadian surveillance overreach

**Slug:** `bill-c-22-canadian-surveillance-history-tommy-douglas-pipeline-protesters`
**Category:** `Accountability`
**Angle:** the historical-Canadian-precedent piece. Shows from
declassified files, CBC reporting, and BCCLA litigation that
surveillance powers granted to RCMP and CSIS have been used against
politicians, Indigenous land defenders, and environmental activists.
Not hypothetical — documented. The article does not claim C-22 will

## Headline

> Canada Has Granted Surveillance Powers Like Bill C-22 Before. Here Is How They Were Used.

## Subheadline

> Declassified RCMP files show the man who built Medicare was surveilled for 30 years. Internal CSIS documents released through the BCCLA show pipeline-protest organizers were monitored, with the intelligence shared back to the company being protested. The MMIWG inquiry found Indigenous land defenders were "surveilled, criminalized, and subjected to violence" by the RCMP. Each of these powers, at the time they were granted, had reasonable-sounding justifications. Here is what the record shows.

## Smart Brevity

- **bigThing**: The argument that "surveillance powers will only be used against the right targets" is a claim Canadians have heard before — for over 50 years. The declassified record shows otherwise.
- **whyItMatters**: Bill C-22's defenders point to safeguards and to limited use cases. The historical record is what happens after those safeguards are in place, when the political context shifts.
- **goDeeper**:
  - The RCMP surveilled Tommy Douglas for 30+ years (1939–1986), keeping him in their VIP-program dossier alongside 650 other politicians and public figures.
  - RCMP ran an "Indigenous extremism program" from 1968 to 1982; CSIS restarted similar surveillance in 1988.
  - CSIS may have falsely told police that Indigenous land defenders at Ipperwash were armed in 1995, days before OPP shot Dudley George.
  - BCCLA litigation forced the release of "Protest Papers" showing CSIS spied on peaceful Northern Gateway pipeline opponents and shared intel with Enbridge.
  - The RCMP commissioner publicly regretted the Indigenous surveillance program in 2024.
- **yesBut**: None of these abuses arose under powers exactly equivalent to Bill C-22; they arose under different statutes, different oversight regimes, and in different eras.
- **bottomLine**: When new powers are granted to RCMP and CSIS, the historical pattern is that the powers expand in practice beyond their stated purpose. Bill C-22 grants new powers with weaker oversight than its predecessors. The pattern of expansion has, every prior time, been documented after the fact — not predicted in advance.

## Sections (outline)

1. **The premise** — when surveillance bills are debated, defenders say "trust the safeguards" and "only specific targets." Canadians have heard this before. The record of what was actually done, after the fact and through declassification, is the test.

2. **Tommy Douglas, 1939–1986** — declassified RCMP files released in 2006 and 2010 show RCMP Security Service surveilled the man who built Medicare for over 30 years. RCMP attended his speeches, dissected his published writing, eavesdropped on a private conversation during a Parliament Hill demonstration. His file is part of a "VIP program" that included 650 politicians and public figures. About one-fifth of the Douglas file remains classified today; CSIS has refused court orders to release the rest. Sources: CBC declassified-files coverage, *Globe and Mail* file release coverage.

3. **The Indigenous-extremism programs, 1968–present** — RCMP ran an "Indigenous extremism" surveillance program from 1968 to 1982. In 1978 it was narrowed to focus on "foreign involvement" in Indigenous rights movements. CSIS restarted the program in 1988. Internal documents indicate that in 1995, CSIS told police that Indigenous land defenders at Ipperwash were armed — a claim that turned out to be false. Three days later, an OPP officer fatally shot land defender Dudley George. The RCMP commissioner publicly apologized in 2024 for the program. Sources: CBC News (Indigenous-surveillance-files series), Chiefs of Ontario statement.

4. **The MMIWG inquiry's findings** — the 2019 National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls found that Indigenous land defenders "are vilified, surveilled, criminalized, and subjected to violence by the RCMP." This is in a formal Royal Commission report, not opinion journalism.

5. **The Northern Gateway pipeline protests, 2013–2017** — BCCLA filed a complaint in 2014 alleging CSIS and RCMP surveilled peaceful protest organizers. After litigation, BCCLA obtained the "Protest Papers" via court order. The released documents (heavily redacted) show: monitoring of social media, attendance at organizing meetings, monitoring of a storytelling workshop at a church in Kelowna. Most damning: surveillance details were shared by Natural Resources Canada with Enbridge (the pipeline operator being protested) and the broader petroleum industry at an industry briefing. Sources: BCCLA Protest Papers release, *National Observer* coverage, *The Narwhal* on the leaked RCMP "violent anti-petroleum extremists" assessment.

6. **The "Native extremism program" Manitoba files** — CBC reporting in early 2026 surfaced detailed RCMP files on First Nations leaders and activists in Manitoba as part of an extended "Native extremism" surveillance project. These files cover figures Canadians would recognize as legitimate political voices.

7. **What this means for Bill C-22** — the bill grants new powers with weaker oversight than its predecessors. The historical record shows that what gets monitored expands beyond stated purposes, that intelligence gets shared with parties whose interests are adverse to those being surveilled (industry, the OPP at Ipperwash), and that the surveillance footprint persists for decades — sometimes 50+ years — beyond the supposed operational need.

8. **What this article is not claiming** — fair-rendering paragraph. The article does not claim: (a) that the Carney government has any intention to misuse C-22; (b) that the RCMP or CSIS are currently planning equivalent abuses; (c) that historical abuses prove future ones. It does claim that the historical record is what should inform Parliamentary debate over C-22's oversight structure — not the assurances of the day.
