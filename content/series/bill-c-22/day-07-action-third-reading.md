# Day 7 (Sun) — How to push back before third reading

**Category:** `Accountability`

## Headline

> Bill C-22 Is at Committee. Here Are the Five Things That Move a Bill at This Stage.

## Subheadline

> Once a bill passes second reading, the next stage is committee study — the only realistic window for substantive amendments. Here is what the Standing Committee on Public Safety and National Security does, when its hearings happen, who sits on it, and how to make sure your perspective is on the record before third reading.

## Smart Brevity

- **bigThing**: Bill C-22 is at the Standing Committee on Public Safety and National Security. Committee is the last stage where substantive amendments are realistic before the final House vote.
- **whyItMatters**: Once the bill clears committee and returns to the House for third reading, the Liberal majority can pass it without further changes. Committee is where the public's window of influence is widest.
- **goDeeper**: Five things move a bill at committee — public submissions, witness testimony, MP constituency mail, media editorials, and inter-party amendment negotiation. Each has a specific access path.
- **yesBut**: Even highly active committee participation does not guarantee amendments. The government decides what to accept. But the historical record shows that high-profile committee opposition has changed bills in the past — including the Toews lawful-access bill in 2012.
- **bottomLine**: Civic action at committee is documented to work. The pathway is concrete. Below is the toolkit.

## Sections (outline)

1. **The committee, in plain English** — Standing Committee on Public Safety and National Security (SECU). 10 voting members: by convention 6 Liberal, 3 opposition (CPC and others), 1 chair. Studies the bill clause by clause, hears witnesses, votes on amendments, reports the bill back to the House.

2. **The committee timeline (estimated)** — committee schedules are set by motion. Realistic estimate for C-22: 4–8 weeks of witness hearings starting in May 2026, then 2–4 weeks of clause-by-clause review, then a report back to the House. Total: 6–12 weeks at committee.

3. **The five things that move a bill at committee:**

   1. **Public submissions to the committee.** Anyone can submit a written brief. SECU accepts briefs via the parliamentary committee submission portal. Briefs become public record and are entered into the official committee evidence.
   2. **Witness testimony.** The committee invites witnesses. To be invited, an individual or organization typically needs to be either (a) a recognized subject-matter expert; (b) a representative of an affected stakeholder; or (c) requested by an MP on the committee. Civil-society groups, academics, providers, OPC officials, and law-enforcement representatives are typical witnesses on lawful-access bills.
   3. **MP constituency mail.** Members of Parliament receive and tabulate constituent letters. For bills with active public debate, MP offices brief their members on constituent reactions. This is most effective when directed to: (a) Liberal MPs on the SECU committee, (b) the bill's sponsor (Public Safety Minister), and (c) Liberal backbench MPs who may be uncomfortable with parts of the bill.
   4. **Media editorials.** Major-outlet editorials directly influence committee study by shaping the questions MPs ask in hearings and by elevating expert voices in witness lists. Globe and Mail, *La Presse*, CBC, *Toronto Star*, and *National Post* editorial boards have historically engaged on lawful-access bills.
   5. **Inter-party amendment negotiation.** The committee's whip and chair conduct quiet negotiations between government and opposition over which amendments will be accepted. Opposition amendments require some Liberal support to pass.

4. **How to do each one:**

   - **Submit a brief:** the committee's portal accepts written submissions. Format: plain-text or PDF, header with name + affiliation + date, body addressing specific provisions of the bill. Length: any (briefs from 1 page to 50 pages are accepted).
   - **Contact your MP:** every MP has a House of Commons email and a riding office. The most effective constituent letters are: specific to a particular provision (e.g., "the secret capability order in section X"), short, and from a constituent of that MP.
   - **Reach out to organizations submitting briefs:** if you don't have time to submit your own, support an organization that is. Donate, volunteer, or amplify their work. OpenMedia, BCCLA, CCLA, the Citizen Lab, and individual academic researchers are all expected to engage with C-22.
   - **Find your MP:** parliamentaudit.ca has a [Find Your MP](https://parliamentaudit.ca/find-your-mp) tool. ourcommons.ca also lists MP contact information.

5. **How to track the committee in real time:**

   - Committee meetings are streamed at <https://parlvu.parl.gc.ca/>.
   - Transcripts are published the following day at <https://www.ourcommons.ca/Committees/en/SECU>.
   - LEGISinfo tracks the bill's status: <https://www.parl.ca/legisinfo/en/bill/45-1/c-22>.
   - Parliament Audit will continue to cover material updates here at parliamentaudit.ca.

6. **The historical precedent that suggests this works** — Bill C-30 (the 2012 "Lawful Access Act" tabled by Public Safety Minister Vic Toews) was withdrawn by the Conservative government after sustained committee testimony, expert opposition (Geist, OPC, BCCLA, Citizen Lab), and constituent pressure. The current Bill C-22 is, in some ways, a narrower version of C-30 — narrower because of how the 2012 opposition shaped what was politically possible. The committee stage is where that kind of shaping happens.

7. **A note on what this article doesn't do** — Parliament Audit takes no position on whether C-22 should pass. We report what's in the bill, what its critics and supporters say, and what the civic-action pathways are. The reader decides what to do with that.
