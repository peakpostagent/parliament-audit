# Parliament Audit — Monetization Strategy

**Date:** April 2026
**Status:** Working doc, year-1 framing
**Positioning constraint:** Non-partisan, accountability-first civic journalism. Revenue must not compromise that.

---

## TL;DR

At <100 followers and 6 months in, the only revenue paths that matter this year are: (1) a low-friction tip jar, (2) grant applications to Canadian journalism funds, and (3) seeding a paid tier you can activate at 1K followers. B2B data licensing is the highest-ceiling path, but depends on publishing the vote pipeline first. Ignore sponsorships, merch, and affiliates until 2027.

---

## 1. Revenue Channels Ranked by Year-1 Realism

Ranked by effort-adjusted ROI at the current 0–5K-follower stage.

### Tier A — Do now (weeks, not months)

1. **Direct donations via Ko-fi or Stripe.** Ko-fi has no platform fee on one-time tips, keeps 5% on memberships, and takes minutes to set up. Patreon takes 8–12% and only pays off above ~$600/mo. At <100 followers a tip jar realistically produces $0–$50/mo, but the button must exist so it compounds. Add "Support us" in the site footer, newsletter footer, and social bios.

2. **Grant applications — Journalism Futures Fund (Inspirit + McConnell).** Inspirit's Journalism Futures Fund awarded its inaugural cohort in February 2026: $1.875M across several outlets, three-year flexible grants of $50K–$140K/year. It explicitly targets "independent small- and medium-sized publications." Organizational Funding stream opened fall 2025; Project-Based Funding stream is being announced through 2026. This is the single most important application to prepare. Requires incorporated entity and a clear editorial mission doc.

3. **Founding-supporter tier (pre-paywall).** Don't launch a paywall yet — ask for founding members at $5/mo or $50/yr on Ghost or Substack. At 1K newsletter subscribers with a 3% conversion (the honest benchmark for journalism, not Substack's 5–10% pitch; political newsletters trend higher, 4–7%+), that's 30 paid × $50 = $1.5K/yr. Not a business yet, but signal you need for grant applications.

### Tier B — Start groundwork now, activate at 1K followers

4. **Local Journalism Initiative (LJI).** Up to $60K/year for one full-time reporter position; bridge funding extended to Jan 31, 2026 and the program continues through 2026–27 including the Changing Narratives Fund stream. Eligibility: press agency, private news organization, or non-profit news organization producing original civic journalism for underserved communities. Parliament Audit's pitch here is weak-to-moderate — "underserved communities" typically means geographic or linguistic, not thematic (parliamentary accountability). Worth an exploratory email to lji@newsmediacanada.ca before spending a week on the application.

5. **Canadian Journalism Collective (CJC-CCJ) — Online News Act funding.** $100M/year from Google, ~$13,798 per full-time-equivalent journalist, distributed to 450+ verified outlets. **Blocker for Parliament Audit:** requires two full-time employees. Freelancers don't count. This is a 2027 target, not a 2026 one.

6. **Canada Periodical Fund — Special Measures for Journalism.** Budget 2025 allocated $38.4M over three years starting 2026–27 for small and community media. Lower ceiling per outlet, less selective than LJI. Track announcements on Canadian Heritage pages.

### Tier C — Real money, but later

7. **B2B data licensing.** This is the sleeper path. Canadian government-relations firms (Earnscliffe, Crestview, Enterprise, McMillan Vantage, StrategyCorp, Hill+Knowlton) pay real money for structured legislative intelligence. Hill Times subscriptions run $400–$600/yr per seat and GR shops buy dozens. iPolitics pricing is similar. **The moat for Parliament Audit:** structured, queryable vote data with member-level accountability scoring that isn't trivially replicable from OurCommons.ca. Estimated pricing: $500–$2,000/seat/year for a dashboard, $5K–$25K/year for API access, $10K+ for custom reports. Needs the vote pipeline published and 3–6 months of coverage first. Target 2027.

8. **Licensing content to local news outlets.** Weekly MP accountability columns syndicated to community papers at $50–$200/column. Only viable once you have a steady output and one sample piece that clearly does what a local paper wants (their MP, their ridings).

### Tier D — Probably don't bother

9. **Merch.** Caps/stickers/totes produce <$500/yr at under 5K followers and consume emotional bandwidth. Revisit at 25K.
10. **Affiliate revenue (Indigo/Amazon books on Canadian politics).** A few hundred dollars a year at most, adds cognitive clutter to the site.
11. **Sponsored civic awareness.** High ethics risk for non-partisan positioning. Skip.
12. **Speaking/consulting.** Will come to you later; no outbound needed now.

## 3. Realistic Earnings Projections

Benchmarks from comparable Canadian outlets, plus honest estimates for Parliament Audit's trajectory.

### Comparable outlet data points

- **Canadaland.** ~5,600 Patreon supporters after 7 years. Late-2018 transparency: 5,408 Patreon subs, ~$27K/mo. Subscriptions are roughly half their revenue; rest is ads + crowdfunding. In 2024 they migrated Patreon subs to Supercast. *Takeaway: 7 years to hit $325K/yr subscription revenue with a marquee host and a popular podcast.*
- **The Logic.** Doubled paid subs in 2024, 270% revenue growth since 2019, subscriptions at $300/yr. Private numbers, but reasonable estimate is 5K–10K paid subs = $1.5M–$3M/yr. *Takeaway: B2B-priced subscriptions scale fast if you have must-read content for professionals.*
- **The Hub.** Founded 2021, ~10M unique web users cumulative, subs from $10/yr, funded by subscriptions + advertisers + Munk's Centre for Civic Engagement. *Takeaway: institutional backing closes the gap between follower count and revenue.*
- **The Line.** Substack-based, "tens of thousands" of subs, two marquee bylines (Gurney, Gerson). Reasonable estimate: 2K–5K paid at $50–$80/yr = $100K–$400K/yr split two ways. *Takeaway: Substack political conversion rates (4–7%+) beat the general 2–5% benchmark.*

### Parliament Audit projections

## 4. Legal / Tax Structure — Questions for an Accountant

This section is **not legal advice.** These are the questions to take to a Canadian accountant or media lawyer (Cyndi Kirkhope at CAJ has a referral list; Dentons, Stikeman and Torys each have media practices).

### When to incorporate

- Below ~$15K/yr revenue, sole-proprietor is fine. Keep receipts.
- At the point of applying for a grant from Journalism Futures Fund, LJI, or Canada Periodical Fund: **most require an incorporated entity** (federal or provincial corporation, or non-profit).
- A federal for-profit corporation is ~$200 to register online. A federal non-profit is ~$250 but has ongoing governance obligations (members, board, AGM).

### Registered Journalism Organization (RJO) — CRA

RJOs are qualified donees since 2020. Benefits:
- Can issue tax receipts for donations (big unlock — doubles effective donation value for Canadian supporters)
- Can receive gifts from registered charities
- Eligible for the Canadian Journalism Labour Tax Credit (35% of qualifying labour expenditures through Dec 2026, 25% after)

Requirements:
- Must be corporation or trust
- Must be primarily engaged in original news content
- Arm's-length board; no single controller
- **No single donor can represent >20% of revenue in a year** (kills the "one big anchor donor" path)
- Must disclose donors >$5K publicly

**Plain English:** Becoming an RJO is the unlock that turns donations into tax-deductible giving. Worth pursuing once revenue crosses ~$25K/yr and an incorporated non-profit structure is in place.

### Qualified Canadian Journalism Organization (QCJO)

Separate from RJO — a designation under Income Tax Act for organizations that let subscribers claim the Digital News Subscription Tax Credit (15% of the subscription cost). Ghost, Substack and custom Stripe subs can all be made QCJO-eligible. Pursue alongside RJO.

### Elections Canada — Third Party

Good news: Elections Canada explicitly exempts **"media activities such as news, editorials and interviews conducted by ongoing media organizations"** from third-party registration, even if coverage could be perceived to favour one party. Parliament Audit's normal editorial output is exempt. The boundary to watch: don't buy ads *targeting* voters during a pre-election or election period — that crosses into regulated activity at $500 of combined spend.

### CJC-CCJ membership

Not a legal requirement, but membership in the Canadian Journalism Collective is the gatekeeper for Online News Act funding (the Google $100M pool). Won't qualify until there are 2 FTE employees.

**Action items for the accountant call:**
1. Incorporate federally as a non-profit vs. for-profit — which better preserves grant eligibility without sacrificing operational flexibility?
2. Sequencing: incorporate → QCJO → RJO — which path, in what order, given current revenue?
3. GST/HST registration threshold ($30K/yr revenue) — plan ahead.

## 5. Ethical Conflicts — Clear Lines

Non-partisan accountability is the entire brand. Revenue that compromises it is worth zero, even if the cheque cashes.

### Hard no

- **Political party ad buys, donations, or sponsorships** — including riding associations, third-party advocacy groups that are effectively partisan (e.g. Canada Proud, North99, Canadian Taxpayers Federation when running campaigns).
- **Lobbyist firms as data customers for targeted opposition research** — GR firms as *subscribers* to a published product is fine (they subscribe to Hill Times, iPolitics, The Logic too). Custom "help us oppose X" contracts are not.
- **Grants from a sitting government's discretionary funds** that could imply editorial favour. Arms-length, formulaic programs (LJI, CJC) are fine because every eligible outlet gets roughly the same deal. One-off ministerial allocations are not.
- **Cryptocurrency / gambling / binary options sponsorships** — reputation risk dwarfs any payment.
- **Single donor >20% of revenue** — also the RJO rule. Enforce it regardless of RJO status.

### Proceed with disclosure

- **Foundation grants** (Inspirit, McConnell, Euphrosine, Sonor) — disclose on an "About our funding" page. Canadaland publishes a disclosure page; use it as a template.
- **Subscribers from GR firms, parties, government offices** — fine, standard, don't even need to disclose individually (every outlet has these).
- **Academic partnerships** — disclose; co-authorship is fine.
- **Advertising** — only from non-political brands, and only where clearly labelled. Skip until there's enough audience to be choosy.

### Grey zone — decide case by case

- **Union or industry-association sponsorships** (e.g. CUPE, Canadian Chamber, Canadian Federation of Independent Business). These groups have policy positions. Accepting money and then covering their files is a credibility hit. Default to no.
- **Think-tank partnerships** (C.D. Howe, Broadbent, Macdonald-Laurier, IRPP). Accept research collaborations and data sharing; decline direct sponsorships.

## 6. 90-Day Action Plan

Ranked by effort-adjusted ROI. Each step has a concrete output.

### Weeks 1–2 (April 21 – May 4)

- [ ] **Set up Ko-fi account** with a Parliament Audit tip jar and $5/$15/$50 suggested amounts. Est. time: 90 minutes.
- [ ] **Add "Support" links** to site footer, newsletter footer, X bio, Bluesky bio, and a dedicated /support page explaining where money goes.
- [ ] **Draft a one-page funding + editorial-independence statement** — the "where our money comes from" page. Will be needed for every grant application.
- [ ] **Email lji@newsmediacanada.ca** with a 150-word pitch: "Parliament Audit covers MP voting behaviour for Canadians underserved by mainstream parliamentary coverage. Is a federally-focused accountability beat eligible under LJI?" Logs the relationship; the answer shapes Q2 effort.

### Weeks 3–4 (May 5 – May 18)

- [ ] **Incorporate.** Federal non-profit (Corporations Canada, ~$250, online, ~5 business days). Pick three directors in advance. The incorporation is the unlock for every grant.
- [ ] **Open a business bank account** (RBC, Scotiabank and Alterna all do free non-profit accounts).
- [ ] **Ship the vote pipeline to production.** Every grant application gets stronger once there is a public product, not just articles. This is the single highest-leverage engineering task this quarter.

### Weeks 5–8 (May 19 – June 15)

- [ ] **Prepare Journalism Futures Fund application** (when Project-Based stream opens in 2026). Key artifacts needed: mission doc, 12-month editorial plan, governance structure, 3-year budget, theory of change. Budget 15–25 hours of writing; reuse for every future grant.
- [ ] **Register as a Qualified Canadian Journalism Organization (QCJO)** with CRA. This is the predicate for Digital News Subscription Tax Credit eligibility and the first step toward RJO. Minimum 6-month processing window; start now.
- [ ] **Book a 1-hour call with a Canadian media-sector accountant.** Ask the three questions in Section 4. Budget $300–$500.

### Weeks 9–13 (June 16 – July 20)

- [ ] **Launch a "founding supporter" tier.** $50/yr or $5/mo, no paywall yet — just early access to the vote-tracker dashboard and a monthly backstory email. Target 20 founding members from existing network (friends, family, prior colleagues, any journalist contacts).
- [ ] **One syndication pitch.** Write one MP-specific vote-accountability column, pitch it free to three community papers (Tyee, The Narwhal for issue-specific, or a local weekly in a riding with an interesting voter). Goal: establish a syndication relationship worth $50–$200/column.
- [ ] **Apply for an academic partnership** — one email to Munk School's Centre for Civic Engagement and one to Carleton Journalism asking about data-journalism collaboration on vote pipeline. No cash but major credibility signal for later grant applications.
- [ ] **Join CAJ** ($180/yr). Gets you the funding-resources directory, professional liability awareness, and a byline credential for grant apps.

### End of 90 days — success metrics

- Incorporated non-profit with a business bank account
- Ko-fi live, 5–15 supporters, $100–$400 in the jar
- QCJO application filed
- Journalism Futures Fund application drafted (whether submitted depends on stream opening)
- LJI eligibility question answered yes/no
- Vote pipeline published with at least one month of public data
- 20 founding supporters at ~$50/yr = $1K committed

Everything beyond 90 days is a function of audience growth and the vote pipeline launch. Revisit this doc after the 90-day mark.
