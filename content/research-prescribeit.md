## Executive summary

On **April 27, 2026**, four Conservative MPs (Dan Mazier, Burton Bailey, Helena Konanz, Matt Strauss) formally asked the Auditor General of Canada to investigate **PrescribeIT**, the federally funded national e-prescribing program operated by **Canada Health Infoway** and built by **Telus Health**. Bloc Québécois MP Maxime Blanchette-Joncas backed the call. The program officially shuts down at **11:59 p.m. EST, May 29, 2026** after spending **roughly $290–$298 million** of federal money — a figure the Conservatives round to "more than $300 million."

The committee story is one of **obstruction, not closed-door voting**. Per the Conservative Party's own April 27 statement and CBC's coverage, Liberal MPs **filibustered** committee efforts to release PrescribeIT contribution agreements and performance documents. Mazier publicly warned that Liberals were poised to **restructure the committee membership to reflect their new House majority** (the four Conservative floor crossings plus three byelections), and that once that happened "the public may never see" the documents. A Standing Order 106(4) emergency meeting was called for **Tuesday, April 28, 2026** to elect a new chair and continue the PrescribeIT investigation; that meeting was **listed as televised, not in camera**.

The user's understanding that "the Liberals used their new majority to vote the meeting in camera" is **not corroborated by available reporting or by the HESA notice/minutes for Meetings 29, 30 and 31**. The substantive obstruction is real and reportable; the specific "cameras off" mechanism is not. We can absolutely write the article — but we have to frame it accurately.

## A. The PrescribeIT spending

### Top-line figure
- **Initial budget (2016):** $40 million.
- **Federal spending Health Canada has acknowledged:** "more than $290 million" (Globe and Mail, April 21–22, 2026, citing Health Canada).
- **Figure used by Conservative MPs in their AG letter:** **$298 million** (also used by The Deep Dive, BNN Bloomberg).
- **Headline figure used by CBC, the Globe and most early outlets:** "$250 million" (the conservative early estimate).
- **Mazier's rhetorical figure, propagating in social and CPC messaging:** "more than $300 million over the last 10 years" — used directly in his April 27 press conference ("So what did Canadians get for their $300 million? Well, that's the $300-million question").
- **Annual operating cost in recent years:** approximately **$35 million per year** (Globe and Mail).

**Recommended editorial figure:** "nearly $300 million" or "$298 million," sourced to Health Canada and the Globe. Treat the "$300 million" round number as Conservative framing and attribute it.

### Spending breakdown (what we know vs. what is hidden)
- **Telus Health share:** approximately **$98 million** since 2017 — confirmed by the Globe and Mail April 21–22 reporting and reaffirmed by Telus VP Ratcho Batchvarov in committee testimony.
- **Remaining spending (~$200 million):** **not centrally tracked** by Health Canada. CEO Michael Green told the April 21 health committee hearing that detailed PrescribeIT spending data "is not centrally tracked by Health Canada" — a quote that is itself part of the accountability story. Infoway promised follow-up board reports but had not delivered them as of April 22.
- **Federal-Provincial-Territorial (FPT) cost-sharing:** **never agreed.** Per Health Minister spokesperson Guillaume Bertrand, the absence of an FPT cost-sharing agreement was the deciding factor in cancellation. Provinces declined to fund operating costs.
- **Pharmacist user fee (2025):** **$0.20 per prescription**, introduced when federal funding was reduced. The Canadian Pharmacists Association said in a December 2024 statement that 65% of pharmacists in their survey would abandon PrescribeIT if the fee was introduced. Many did.

### Adoption rates
- **Prescriptions sent through PrescribeIT in any given year:** **less than 5%** of all prescriptions in Canada, despite signing on Shoppers Drug Mart, Metro Ontario, Walmart, and thousands of clinics.
- Most Canadian prescriptions in 2026 are still sent by **fax or paper**.
- CEO Michael Green's exact testimony (April 21): "The ease of adoption by primary care physicians and prescribers was lower than expected."

### Vendor structure
- **Builder:** Telus Health, a subsidiary of Telus Corp. Designed and built the platform and assigned roughly 50 employees to it.
- **IP ownership:** **Telus retains approximately 85% of the intellectual property** underlying PrescribeIT. Health Canada confirmed in an order paper response that **"the government holds no intellectual property pertaining to PrescribeIT."**
- **Vendor lock-in:** Telus VP Ratcho Batchvarov told the committee the program "couldn't be transferred or maintained without Telus's involvement."

### Provincial coverage
- **At peak:** PrescribeIT operated in **eight provinces and territories**, with **Quebec in a trial phase**.
- Specific provincial breakdowns by adoption rate are **not in the public reporting** we located.

## B. The committee meetings and the "in camera" question

### What we know for certain about HESA meetings on PrescribeIT
**Meeting 29 — Tuesday, April 21, 2026, 3:38 p.m. – 7:19 p.m. EDT**
- Topic: "Briefing on PrescribeIT"
- Format: **Webcast** (public)
- Witnesses: Canada Health Infoway CEO Michael Green; Health Canada officials; Telus Health (Ratcho Batchvarov, VP Provider Solutions)
- Outcome: Committee voted **unanimously** to summon Green again, Infoway's Board Chair, and a Telus Health representative for further testimony. Deadline set for May 6, 2026.
- Notable Liberal questions on record:
  - Hedy Fry (then-Chair, Liberal): "Can you tell us what was wrong?"
  - Doug Eyolfson (Liberal): "Why weren't physicians using this? Had problems been identified?"
- Notable Conservative questions:
  - Helena Konanz asked Green to disclose his salary; he declined.

**Meeting 30 — Thursday, April 23, 2026, 3:30 p.m. – 6:42 p.m. EDT**
- Topic: "Canada's Pharmaceutical Sovereignty" (NOT PrescribeIT)
- Format: **Webcast** (public)

**Meeting 31 — Tuesday, April 28, 2026, 12:00 p.m. – 1:00 p.m. EDT**
- Type: **Standing Order 106(4) opposition-requested meeting**
- Notice (per ourcommons.ca): "Meeting Requested Pursuant to Standing Order 106(4) **to Discuss the Committee's Investigation on PrescribeIT Program**"
- Agenda items: (1) **Election of Chair**; (2) the PrescribeIT investigation
- Format on the notice: **Televised** (public). The notice does not list any in-camera segment.
- Location: Room 035-B, West Block
- **As of this brief's compilation (evening of April 28, 2026), the official minutes for Meeting 31 were not yet published on ourcommons.ca.** The video of the proceedings is hosted at parlvu.parl.gc.ca but we have not been able to verify its contents through automated tools.

### What we cannot confirm
- **No reporting we located describes a vote to go in camera on PrescribeIT.** Multiple Canadian outlets (CBC, Globe and Mail, BNN Bloomberg, The Deep Dive, The Canadian Press) published April 27–28 stories about the AG letter and the Liberal obstruction; **none describe an in-camera vote.**
- The Conservative Party's own April 27 press release describes the obstruction in different terms: **"Instead of allowing a vote, Liberal Members filibustered the health committee to block the release of those documents."** Filibustering is not the same as moving in camera. A filibuster runs out the clock on a committee meeting; an in-camera motion converts a public meeting into a private one with no transcript published.
- We did not locate any social media post from Mazier, Bailey, Konanz, Strauss or Blanchette-Joncas describing an in-camera vote on PrescribeIT.

### What the actual obstruction looks like
Per Mazier's April 27 press conference (paraphrased and quoted in CBC, the Globe, BNN Bloomberg, the CPC press release, The Canadian Press):

> Conservatives have been working at the committee level to produce documents related to PrescribeIT. The government has been **filibustering** those efforts. Liberals are expected to **restructure the parliamentary committees this week to reflect their new majority in the House of Commons.** If that happens, "the public may never see documents explaining the government's handling of PrescribeIT."

> "Most Canadians have never heard of PrescribeIT. And that's exactly how the Liberals wanted it." — Mazier, April 27 press conference.

The **Election of Chair item on Meeting 31 (April 28)** is consistent with the committee restructuring Mazier warned about. Hedy Fry (Liberal, Vancouver Centre) chaired Meeting 29 on April 21. If the Liberals have used their new House majority to swap committee membership and elect a new chair — that IS a power-shift story, even if it's not the closed-door vote framing the user expected.

### Liberal MPs on HESA (reference)
The April 21 HESA membership in the 45-1 session included Liberal MPs Hedy Fry (Chair, Vancouver Centre) and Doug Eyolfson, with a Liberal majority on the committee tracking the post-floor-crossings House majority. The full updated post-restructuring membership was not yet published on ourcommons.ca/Committees/en/HESA/Members at compile time.

### Press coverage of obstruction
- **CBC News, April 27, 2026:** Reports Mazier's filibustering allegation; does not describe an in-camera vote.
- **Globe and Mail, April 27, 2026:** Reports the committee will meet Tuesday on an emergency motion; does not describe an in-camera vote.
- **The Deep Dive, April 27, 2026:** Quotes "$298M" figure; does not describe an in-camera vote.
- **BNN Bloomberg / The Canadian Press, April 27, 2026:** Reports the AG letter and the filibuster characterization.
- **Conservative Party of Canada press release, April 27, 2026:** Uses the word "filibustered," not "in camera."

## C. Auditor General and PBO involvement

### Auditor General
- **Status:** No AG report on PrescribeIT or Canada Health Infoway has been published.
- **April 27, 2026:** Four Conservative MPs (Mazier, Bailey, Konanz, Strauss) sent a formal letter to the Auditor General requesting an investigation. **Bloc support** from Maxime Blanchette-Joncas (vice-chair of HESA).
- **The AG's office has not, as of April 28 evening, publicly committed to a probe.** The AG's 2025-26 Departmental Plan commits to roughly 25 performance audits but does not name PrescribeIT.
- The full text of the Conservative letter to the AG is **not in the public reporting** — only summaries.

### Parliamentary Budget Officer (PBO)
- **No PBO report dedicated to Canada Health Infoway or PrescribeIT** has been published in 2025 or 2026 based on our search of pbo-dpb.ca publications. PBO has reported on other federal health programs (Interim Federal Health Program, Canada Health Transfer) but Infoway is not a current PBO subject.
- This is itself a notable gap. Infoway has received roughly $2.4 billion+ in cumulative federal funding since 2001 without a recent PBO costing review.

## D. The accountability angle

### Who has been asked to testify (and what they've said)
- **Canada Health Infoway CEO Michael Green:** appeared April 21, 2026. Refused to disclose his salary when asked. Promised follow-up board reports; not delivered as of April 22.
- **Canada Health Infoway Board Chair:** **summoned** by unanimous committee vote April 21, but **not yet named in available reporting** and not yet appeared.
- **Telus Health:** Ratcho Batchvarov (VP Provider Solutions) testified April 21. Confirmed Telus retains ~85% of IP and that no transfer is possible without Telus.
- **Health Minister Marjorie Michel:** has **not personally testified or made a major public statement on PrescribeIT.** Comment so far has been routed through her spokesperson Guillaume Bertrand.

### Watchdog and stakeholder reaction
- **Canadian Pharmacists Association (CEO Glen Doucet):** publicly opposed the $0.20 transaction fee in December 2024 ("Pharmacies are being asked to absorb the cost of a solution that benefits the entire health-care system"); 65% of pharmacists surveyed by CPhA said they'd abandon the service if the fee was implemented. Has now called for "a carrot rather than a stick" approach in any successor program.
- **Canadian Medical Association (CMA):** **no statement located** on PrescribeIT in our search.
- **Democracy Watch / CTF / CCPA / IRPP:** **no specific statement located** on PrescribeIT in our search.
- **NDP:** **not on the record** in available reporting. This is a gap — if Parliament Audit wants balanced opposition coverage, this is the most reachable hole to fill before publication.

### Formal complaints triggered
- The April 27 letter from four Conservative MPs to the Auditor General **is** the formal complaint. There is no separate Information Commissioner or Lobbying Commissioner referral that we located.

## G. Confidence callout for publication

**Overall confidence we can publish a Parliament Audit article without fabrication: HIGH — IF the angle is reframed.**

### What we can write with high confidence
- The $298 million spending figure (with attribution to Health Canada and the Globe).
- Telus retains 85% of IP; federal government holds none.
- Adoption was below 5% of prescriptions.
- Health Canada does not centrally track PrescribeIT spending — Green's own admission.
- Four Conservative MPs (Mazier, Bailey, Konanz, Strauss) plus Bloc's Blanchette-Joncas have asked the AG to investigate.
- Liberals **filibustered** Conservative motions to release contribution agreements and performance documents.
- Liberals were expected to restructure HESA membership to reflect their new House majority; an Election of Chair was held on April 28 under SO 106(4).
- Michael Green declined to disclose his salary to the committee.
- The program ends May 29, 2026.

### What we CANNOT write
- "Liberals voted the meeting in camera" — **no evidence.** The HESA Meeting 31 notice says televised; the April 21 hearing was webcast. The mechanism of obstruction in the public record is **filibustering** plus **committee restructuring**, not in-camera votes.
- "The Auditor General is auditing PrescribeIT" — only that Conservatives have asked.
- "The four floor crossings flipped HESA membership today" — we don't yet have the post-restructuring HESA member list to verify the exact mechanism.

### Recommended reframing
**Drop the "cameras off" framing.** The actual story is stronger and more defensible:

> *Liberals used a parliamentary filibuster to block a committee vote that would have released contribution agreements and performance documents on a $298 million failed e-prescribing program. With their new House majority — secured via four Conservative floor crossings and three byelections — they were then expected to restructure the health committee membership itself. On April 28, the committee elected a new chair under an opposition-forced Standing Order 106(4) emergency meeting on the PrescribeIT investigation.*

That is true, sourced, and (frankly) a sharper accountability story than the in-camera framing.
