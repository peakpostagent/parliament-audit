## 1. Story Identification

The "$300 million health care software" story almost certainly refers to **PrescribeIT**, the federal e-prescribing program run by **Canada Health Infoway** (a federally funded not-for-profit) and built primarily by **Telus Health**.

The story is extremely fresh: on **April 27, 2026** (yesterday), four Conservative MPs on the House of Commons health committee formally asked the Auditor General of Canada to investigate the program. This is the news that almost certainly reached the user. The program was officially cancelled on February 10–18, 2026, will go offline at 11:59 PM EST on **May 29, 2026**, and a parliamentary health committee hearing on April 21–22, 2026 grilled Canada Health Infoway CEO Michael Green over its failure.

**Why "$300 million" specifically:** The program started with a $40-million budget in 2016. Most outlets are now using either the headline figure of **$250 million** (the conservative early estimate) or **$298 million** (federal spending Health Canada has acknowledged). Conservative MP Dan Mazier framed the file as "the $300-million question" in his April 27 press conference, and several outlets (CBC, BNN Bloomberg, The Deep Dive) have rounded up to "more than $300 million over the last 10 years." This is the figure that has propagated into public conversation in the past 24 hours.

**Confidence in identification: HIGH.** No other Canadian health-IT story currently in the news matches this dollar figure or this timing.

## 2. The Fact Sheet

### What it is

- **Project name:** PrescribeIT
- **Tagline used by Ottawa:** "Axe the Fax"
- **Purpose:** A national e-prescribing service intended to transmit prescriptions electronically between physicians and pharmacies, replacing fax machines.
- **Operator:** Canada Health Infoway — a federally funded, not-for-profit corporation. Receives essentially all funding from the Government of Canada.
- **Primary technology vendor:** Telus Health (subsidiary of Telus Corp.). Designed and built the platform; Telus retained ~85% of the underlying intellectual property.
- **Geographic scope at peak:** Operated in eight provinces and territories, with Quebec in a trial phase.

### When

- **2016:** Initial federal announcement; budget set at **$40 million**.
- **2017:** PrescribeIT launched.
- **2018:** Operational rollout.
- **2025:** Federal government begins reducing funding. Canada Health Infoway introduces a **$0.20-per-prescription fee** charged to pharmacists. Several pharmacies abandon the service.
- **February 10, 2026:** The Globe and Mail reports the program is being cancelled.
- **February 18, 2026:** Canada Health Infoway formally announces shutdown.
- **April 21–22, 2026:** House of Commons health committee questions Infoway CEO Michael Green.
- **April 27, 2026:** Four Conservative MPs formally request an Auditor General investigation.
- **May 1, 2026 (or before):** A "national e-prescribing standard" to be made publicly available as a replacement framework.
- **May 6, 2026:** Deadline set by health committee for follow-up testimony.
- **May 29, 2026, 11:59 PM EST:** PrescribeIT goes offline.

### How much

- **Initial budget (2016):** $40 million
- **Total federal spending acknowledged by Health Canada:** "More than $290 million" (Globe and Mail, April 21–22, 2026, citing Health Canada)
- **Total figure cited in audit request:** $298 million (federal spending) — used by Conservative MPs and Globe and Mail
- **Headline figure in early reporting:** "$250 million-plus" (CBC, BNN Bloomberg, Globe — Feb 10, 2026)
- **Telus Health's share:** ~$98 million since 2017
- **Recent annual operating cost:** ~$35 million per year
- **Pharmacist user fee introduced 2025:** $0.20 per prescription
- **Working figure used colloquially in opposition messaging and several outlets:** "More than $300 million over 10 years"

### Why it failed (or why people are upset)

Multiple, well-documented reasons drawn from primary reporting and parliamentary testimony:

1. **Catastrophically low adoption.** Fewer than 5% of prescriptions in Canada were sent through PrescribeIT in any given year, despite thousands of pharmacies (including Shoppers Drug Mart, Metro Ontario, Walmart) and clinics signing up. Most prescriptions are still sent by fax or paper in 2026.
2. **No federal-provincial cost-sharing model.** Per Health Minister spokesperson Guillaume Bertrand, the lack of an FPT cost-sharing agreement was the deciding factor in cancellation. Provinces and territories declined to share operating costs.
3. **The $0.20 pharmacist fee backfired.** When federal funding was cut in 2025, Infoway charged pharmacists per prescription, prompting some to abandon the service. Glen Doucet, CEO of the Canadian Pharmacists Association, called for "more of a carrot than a stick."
4. **Vendor IP retention.** Telus Health retained roughly 85% of the underlying IP. The federal government holds no intellectual property despite funding the build. Conservatives have flagged this as a vendor lock-in concern.
5. **Lower-than-expected ease of physician adoption.** CEO Michael Green told the health committee: "The ease of adoption by primary care physicians and prescribers was lower than expected."
6. **Limited transparency on spending.** Health Canada told MPs that detailed PrescribeIT spending data "is not centrally tracked by Health Canada."

### Who's accountable

- **Federal Minister of Health (current):** **Marjorie Michel** (Carney government, in office since 2025)
- **Minister's spokesperson on file:** Guillaume Bertrand
- **Canada Health Infoway CEO:** **Michael Green** (appeared before health committee April 21–22, 2026)
- **Canada Health Infoway Board Chair:** Summoned to testify; not named in available reporting
- **Primary vendor:** Telus Health (a Telus Corp. subsidiary) — assigned ~50 employees to the program
- **MPs leading the audit request:** Dan Mazier (lead, Conservative health critic), Burton Bailey, Helena Konanz, Matt Strauss — all Conservative members of the House of Commons health committee
- **Cross-party support for audit:** Bloc Québécois MP Maxime Blanchette-Joncas

### Auditor General findings

**No AG report has been published yet.** The Auditor General of Canada has been formally asked (April 27, 2026) to launch an investigation. The AG's office has not, as of this brief's compilation, publicly committed to a probe.

### Public reaction

- **Conservatives (Mazier):** "So what did Canadians get for their $300 million? Well, that's the $300-million question." Mazier separately framed it as: "The $300-million question is simple: Who got rich?"
- **Bloc Québécois (Blanchette-Joncas):** Endorsed the call for an Auditor General investigation.
- **Liberal committee members** asked diagnostic questions during the April 21–22 hearing. Doug Eyolfson: "Why weren't physicians using this? Had problems been identified?" Committee chair Hedy Fry: "Can you tell us what was wrong?"
- **Canadian Pharmacists Association (CEO Glen Doucet):** Critical of the per-prescription fee structure; called for incentives over penalties; supports continued health data standards work.
- **Industry physician voice (Dr. James Owen, quoted in Globe):** "It feels like we are already long overdue for health care to make the transition to secure electronic communication."
- **NDP position:** Not yet on the record in available reporting.

### What we DO NOT know (flagged honestly)

- Exact year-by-year federal spending breakdown.
- Whether the AG has agreed to investigate.
- What the remaining ~$200 million in spending (i.e., not the ~$98M to Telus) was allocated to. Infoway CEO Green promised the committee detailed board reports later but had not provided them as of April 22.
- Whether other vendors received material subcontracts.
- What Michael Green's salary was — Conservative MP Helena Konanz asked; he declined to answer.
- The Canada Health Infoway Board Chair's name.
- Whether Telus Health profited net of costs (the $98M is gross revenue).
- Provincial-by-provincial adoption breakdowns.
- The text of the Conservative MPs' formal letter to the Auditor General (only summarized in news coverage so far).

## 4. Editorial notes for the article writer

- Use **$298 million** (or "nearly $300 million") as the safest hard figure, sourced to Health Canada and Globe and Mail reporting. The "$300 million" framing is rhetorically Conservative; it is fair to attribute the rounded figure to Mazier.
- The accountability story has multiple distinct angles:
  1. **Vendor IP capture** — Ottawa funded the build, Telus owns 85% of the IP.
  2. **No FPT cost-sharing was ever locked in** before launch — a structural policy failure.
  3. **A $0.20-per-prescription fee on pharmacists killed adoption** in the program's final year — a self-inflicted wound.
  4. **Health Canada doesn't centrally track this spending** — a transparency gap.
- Avoid overstating. The Auditor General has not yet announced an investigation. Do not write that "the AG is auditing PrescribeIT" — only that "Conservative MPs have asked the AG to audit PrescribeIT."
- Telus Corp. is a publicly traded Canadian company; financial statements may yield additional verification of the $98M figure if needed.
- No NDP statement is currently on the record. If the article requires balanced opposition coverage, this is a gap to fill before publication.
