# Parliament Audit — Voice Playbook

A field guide for writing posts that sound like a person, not a press release. Non-partisan, fact-first, but unmistakably human.

---

## 1. Voice archetype: the late-shift wire reporter

Pick one archetype and stick to it. Ours is **the late-shift wire reporter at a serious newsroom** — think a Canadian Press or Reuters desk hand who has been watching the chamber for six hours, knows the file cold, and now has to file 280 characters that tell the reader what actually happened. Not a pundit. Not a partisan. Not a "breaking news" screamer. A person with a notebook.

Why this archetype:

- **Authority without opinion.** Wire reporters build credibility by being first and accurate. That is exactly what accountability journalism trades on. The 2025 Reuters Institute Digital News Report found audiences specifically want depth and accountability from reporters, not algorithm-chasing hot takes. A wire voice matches that demand.
- **Compression is a feature, not a limitation.** Wire style was born on the teletype where every character cost money. Twitter and Bluesky have the same constraint. "Simple, direct language that goes straight to the heart of the matter, facts marshalled in logical sequence" (the AP/Reuters house style) is the single most transferable skill to social.
- **Deadpan registers as smart.** Think Philippe Fournier at 338Canada posting "🔴 LPC 189 / majority: 172 seats / 🔵 CPC 123" with no commentary — the numbers are the joke, the alarm, the story. The voice trusts the reader. Matt Levine does this for finance. We do it for Parliament.
- **It scales across formats.** The same voice works for a breaking vote, a $60B budget story, and a floor-crossing contradiction. Punditry archetypes (the columnist, the explainer, the cartoonist) force tonal shifts the brand can't sustain across 300 posts a year.

One-line test: *Would a 15-year CP reporter type this sentence at 11:47pm with a coffee going cold?* If no, rewrite.

## 2. Sentence-level tactics — 18 patterns to use and avoid

**1. Vote results as stories, not scoreboards.**
- BAD: "Bill C-9 passed 186-137."
- GOOD: "Bill C-9 passed tonight — with every NDP MP voting no."

**2. Lead with the human stake, not the procedure.**
- BAD: "Bill C-12 received royal assent on Tuesday."
- GOOD: "30,000 refugee claimants got letters this week. Bill C-12 is why."

**3. Use "we" sparingly and only for work the newsroom did.**
- BAD: "We think this is a big deal."
- GOOD: "We pulled every Hansard entry from the week. Four MPs changed their stated position."

**4. Name sources with specificity, not formality.**
- BAD: "According to official records…"
- GOOD: "From Hansard, Dec 2 at 14:31…"

**5. Time-stamp contradictions like a court exhibit.**
- BAD: "Gladu said one thing and did another."
- GOOD: "Jan 11: Gladu said crossers should face a byelection. Apr 8: she crossed."

**6. Let numbers do the adjective's job.**
- BAD: "A massive budget cut."
- GOOD: "$60.6B over 4 years. 10,000 public service jobs. CRA loses 2,620."

**7. Replace "announced" with the actual verb.**
- BAD: "The Minister announced new legislation."
- GOOD: "The Minister tabled C-22 Monday."

**8. Open with a concrete noun, not a framing word.**
- BAD: "Interesting vote today in the House."
- GOOD: "Four Conservative MPs. Three byelection wins. No general election."

**9. Use sentence fragments for emphasis — sparingly.**
- GOOD: "Same family. Opposite decision. 14 weeks apart."
- (One fragment run per post. More than that and it reads as copywriting.)

**10. Quote the record, don't paraphrase it.**
- BAD: "Ma criticized the Liberals before joining them."
- GOOD: "\"Team feudalism.\" — MP Michael Ma, nine days before he joined them."

**11. Kill every adjective you can defend with a number.**
- BAD: "Wait times will balloon."
- GOOD: "Passport wait times: projected to double by fall."

**12. End on the quietest possible line.**
- BAD: "This is a HUGE story!!"
- GOOD: "She has not called a byelection."

**13. Use "the record" as a recurring house noun.**
- GOOD: "The record: parliamentaudit.ca/…"
- GOOD: "What the record shows:"
- This is our version of the Economist's "We note, however" — a signature phrase that signals the archetype.

**14. Write numbers like a wire desk, not a marketer.**
- BAD: "$60 BILLION in cuts!"
- GOOD: "$60.6B over four years."

**15. Deadpan juxtaposition beats commentary.**
- BAD: "Suspiciously, Jeneroux was rewarded weeks later."
- GOOD: "Nov 6: resigned citing family. Feb 18: crossed. Mar: Special Advisor to the PM."

**16. One clause per fact. Let the reader connect them.**
- BAD: "Even though Ma had called the Liberals feudalist, he joined them anyway, showing…"
- GOOD: "Ma called the Liberals 'team feudalism.' Nine days later, he joined them."

**17. Put the link on its own line with a plain verb.**
- GOOD: "Full breakdown: parliamentaudit.ca/…"
- AVOID: "👉 CLICK HERE 👉"

**18. End the thread, not the sentence, with the CTA.**
- On multi-post threads, the link goes on the final post. Mid-thread links bleed clicks.

## 3. Humanization without opinion — the 4 techniques accountability reporters use

**a) Texture over adjectives.** Philippe Fournier (338Canada) never calls a poll "shocking." He writes "LPC down 4 points in 72 hours" and posts the chart. The texture — the number, the timestamp, the trend — does the emotional work. Apply: every time you reach for an adjective, substitute a number or a timestamp.

**b) The procedural aside.** ProPublica and The Logic routinely use brief asides like "(We asked four times. They did not respond.)" or "(The bill text is 312 pages. This section is on page 287.)" These show *work* without expressing *opinion*. For us: "(We checked Hansard. No such speech exists.)" or "(The budget had not yet been tabled.)"

**c) The named act of journalism.** Saying "We pulled this from Hansard" or "We asked every floor-crosser for comment" is not editorializing — it's disclosure. Canadian Taxpayers Federation does a version of this with "CTF obtained the receipts via FOI." It signals effort, not opinion.

**d) Deadpan juxtaposition.** The Logic and ProPublica both use the "A. B." construction where A and B are both facts and the contradiction is implicit. Example from ProPublica's 2025 impact work: "The company said it had no asbestos. State inspectors had flagged asbestos in 2019, 2021, and 2023." No adjective. No verdict. The reader does the math.

All four techniques show personality — *this newsroom did the work, noticed the pattern, cares enough to check* — without a single partisan syllable.

## 4. What NOT to do — the credibility killers

- **No "BREAKING" unless it genuinely broke in the last 15 minutes.** Reuters' internal guidance: reserve it for things a wire desk would move on the priority wire. Overuse reads as cable-news cosplay.
- **No exclamation points. Ever.** One accidental "!" undoes six months of neutrality positioning.
- **No emoji on every post.** Allowed: a single flag 🍁 on a weekly recap, maybe a ▸ bullet. Not allowed: 🚨🔥💥 or emoji-faces. 338Canada's party-colour circles work because they are a data legend, not decoration.
- **No hashtag-every-noun.** The only acceptable hashtag is #cdnpoli. Never tag parties or leaders.
- **No loaded verbs.** Banned: slams, crushes, guts, destroys, owns, obliterates, betrays, shocks, exposes (when you mean "reports"), blasts, torches, rips.
- **No rhetorical questions.** "Why would anyone vote for this?" is a take. "Who voted for this: [list]" is reporting.
- **No "You won't believe…" or "Wait until you hear…"** These are engagement-bait tells. Credibility erodes instantly.
- **No subtweeting other journalists or accounts.** If we're correcting a claim, quote-post with the record and the source. No vague "some people are saying" posts.
- **No posting votes before the article exists.** The tweet is a promise the link delivers on. Empty links kill trust faster than a bad take.
- **No all-caps for emphasis.** Occasional all-caps is acceptable for the literal name of a procedural item (RECORDED VOTE, ROYAL ASSENT). Never for sentiment.
- **No emoji-bullets substituting for sentences.** 🚨 does not replace a verb.
- **No jokes about MPs' appearance, family, or personal life.** Record only.

## 5. Five rewrites from our current drafts

**Rewrite 1 — Draft 7 (Bill C-9)**

- BEFORE: "Bill C-9 passed the House 186–137. Liberals + Bloc voted yes. Conservatives, NDP, and Greens voted no. The key issue: removing a decades-old religious speech defence."
- AFTER: "Bill C-9 passed the House tonight, 186–137. Every Liberal and Bloc MP voted yes. Every Conservative, NDP, and Green MP voted no. The fault line: a religious-speech defence on the books since the 1970s. What the bill does: parliamentaudit.ca/news/bill-c9-combatting-hate-act-religious-exemption"
- Why: Adds time-stamp ("tonight"), replaces "+" shorthand with human prose, names the fault line instead of "the key issue," keeps the neutral voice.

**Rewrite 2 — Draft 3 ($60B budget)**

- BEFORE: "$60.6B in cuts over 4 years. 10,000 public service jobs eliminated. CRA loses 2,620 positions. Passport wait times projected to double by fall."
- AFTER: "$60.6B in cuts over four years. 10,000 public-service jobs gone. The CRA loses 2,620 positions alone. Passports: projected to double in wait time by fall. Department-by-department: parliamentaudit.ca/news/federal-budget-cuts-60-billion-public-service"
- Why: "Gone" is more human than "eliminated." "Alone" adds texture. Colon-then-detail on passports mimics a wire lede.

**Rewrite 3 — Draft 10 (Bill C-12)**

- BEFORE: "The government can now cancel permanent resident visas, work permits, and study permits if it's deemed in the 'public interest.' That term is not defined in the law."
- AFTER: "Under Bill C-12, the government can now cancel permanent-resident visas, work permits, and study permits in the 'public interest.' The law does not define what 'public interest' means. Full analysis: parliamentaudit.ca/news/bill-c12-immigration-reform-refugee-rights"
- Why: Names the bill up top (context for scrollers). Replaces "That term is not defined" with the stronger active construction. Quotation marks around "public interest" do the skeptical work without an adjective.

**Rewrite 4 — Draft 13 (Gladu)**

- BEFORE: "Jan 11, 2026: MP Marilyn Gladu told the Petrolia Lambton Independent that constituents 'deserve a chance to have a redo' when their MP switches parties. Apr 8, 2026: She crossed the floor to the Liberals. She has not called a byelection."
- AFTER: "Jan 11: MP Marilyn Gladu told the Petrolia Lambton Independent that voters 'deserve a chance to have a redo' when their MP switches parties. Apr 8: she crossed to the Liberals. She has not called a byelection. The record: parliamentaudit.ca/news/marilyn-gladu-byelection-pledge-then-floor-cross"
- Why: Drops the year on the date stamps (reader is in 2026). "Voters" is more human than "constituents." Lowercase "she" after the dash keeps the wire cadence. Ending on "She has not called a byelection." is the quiet-closer move.

**Rewrite 5 — Draft 21 (Floor-crossing roundup)**

- BEFORE: "The 4 Conservative MPs who crossed to the Liberals between Nov 2025 and April 2026: ▸ d'Entremont — won by 533 votes, ethics complaint filed ▸ Ma — called the Liberals 'team feudalism' 9 days before joining them ▸ Jeneroux — said he was resigning citing family, then crossed instead ▸ Gladu — endorsed automatic byelections for crossers, then crossed"
- AFTER: "Four Conservative MPs crossed to the Liberals in the last six months. What each one said on the way out:\n\n▸ d'Entremont — won his seat by 533 votes. Ethics complaint filed.\n▸ Ma — called the Liberals 'team feudalism.' Crossed nine days later.\n▸ Jeneroux — said he was resigning for family. Kept the seat. Crossed.\n▸ Gladu — said crossers should face a byelection. Then crossed.\n\nFull series: parliamentaudit.ca/news"
- Why: Opening sentence is declarative, not a colon-dump. Each bullet now ends on the quiet contradiction. Numbers written as words where they read better ("Four," "nine"). "What each one said on the way out" is voice — wry but non-partisan.

## 6. Implementation recipe — the 5-item pre-post checklist

Before any post goes out, run this:

1. **Adjective audit.** Read the post aloud. Every adjective — can it be replaced with a number, a timestamp, or a quote? If yes, replace it. If no, keep it.
2. **First-line test.** Does the opening line name a concrete thing (a bill number, a dollar figure, a date, a person)? If it opens with a framing word ("Interesting," "Big news," "Today,"), rewrite.
3. **Quiet-close check.** Is the last sentence before the CTA a declarative fact, not an exclamation or a question? If it ends on "!" or "?", rewrite.
4. **Partisan-mirror test.** Swap every party name for the opposite party. Does the post still read as fair? If it now sounds like an attack, you've editorialized. Rewrite.
5. **Link-promise check.** Does the linked article actually deliver what the post implies? Open the URL and read the first paragraph. If there's a gap, fix the post or the article before you post.

If all five pass, ship it.

## 7. The closing CTA — every post points to its sources

Every post that links to a parliamentaudit.ca article ends with the same standardized line:

> **Sources + full breakdown →**

Inserted automatically by both posting scripts (`scripts/browser/post-x-lib.ts` and `scripts/post-arbitrary-bluesky.ts`) between the body and the link. Override per-post with `--cta "<text>"`. Skip with `--no-cta` (rare — usually only for posts that don't link to an article at all).

Why this exact phrasing:
- **"Sources +"** signals our brand pillar (every claim has a source) before "full breakdown" sells the click. Order matters.
- **"+ full breakdown"** preserves the user's original framing ("more detail at the link") in tighter wording.
- **"→"** is a visual cue that the link is *below*, which is true on both X (URL after CTA) and Bluesky (link card under post body).
- **27 chars including the arrow** — leaves enough body budget to fit X's 280 and Bluesky's 300 limits without trimming meaningful content.

What this CTA does NOT say (and why):
- Not "Click here" — generic, flagged as low-effort by both algorithms.
- Not "Read more" — fine, but doesn't reinforce sourcing.
- Not "DM for the article" — closes the open-web loop.
- Not "Subscribe!" — exclamation; we save subscribe pushes for in-thread replies, not link-card posts.

If a post has no article URL (e.g. a stand-alone observation, a quote-of-the-day), skip the CTA entirely. A CTA without a link is just noise.

*Voice is a discipline, not a style. The archetype — late-shift wire reporter — is the anchor. Every tactic above exists to protect it.*
